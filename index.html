We know tech.

With over 15 years of experience building web and mobile products, we are yet to find a problem that we couldn't solve. We have seen it all, from startup to high scale.

  • Optimize through configuration

    Simply declare each type of queries you will perform while configuring your database, and Scalar.ly will organize your data storage to maximize performance and minimize cost.

  • Easily scale your data schema

    No more spending valuable engineering resources writing migration scripts. No more thinking about the best way to store your data. Scalar.ly will automatically handle database migrations for you when your config is updated and automatically optimize the storage patterns as needed.

  • Automatically generate libraries

    After your schema is configured, Scalar.ly will generate models in your static language of choice. Manually writing classes to match your data access layer is a thing of the past. Supported formats: TypeScript & Kotlin.

NoSQL isn't always easy...

It might be simple to get started with NoSQL thanks to the minimal config, but that flexibility often comes back to bite. As a NoSQL application grows, the burden of managing the complexity starts to outweigh the flexibility which is why we see so many mid-sized engineering organizations migrate to SQL. Engineering hours are expensive so leaders invest in order to keep the cognitive overhead of building low.

That works for a while, but at high scale SQL databases suck. With a large dataset, the complex joins will slow to a crawl and the cost of your managed services will explode thanks to all the unnecessary CPU time. The tech titans are more than willing to accept the complexity of the NoSQL approach at scale because the cost savings and performance benefits directly impact their bottom line and their user experience.

With Scalar.ly, you get the best of both worlds: a cost-effective, high performance modern storage solution with the structure of a traditional table-based datastore.

NoSQL gets confused for non-relational. NoSQL data is still relational, it's just denormalized.
